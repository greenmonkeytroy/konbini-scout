# Product Vision — Konbini Scout

## 1. Vision & Mission

### Vision Statement

Every curious snack lover in the Western world has a confident, joyful way to discover and explore Asian snacks — no expertise required, no overwhelm, just a shelf full of things worth trying.

### Mission Statement

Konbini Scout makes Asian snack discovery approachable by letting anyone browse, rate, curate, and share shelves of 5 — turning an overwhelming wall of unfamiliar packaging into a playful, visual experience that feels like walking through a beautifully illustrated convenience store.

### Founder's Why

Troy has been eating, cataloguing, and photographing Asian snacks for over 20 years. That's not a hobby that fades — it's a genuine obsession with the craft, the flavours, and the sheer creativity that goes into Asian snack packaging and product design.

As a creative director, Troy sees what most snack databases get wrong: they treat discovery as a list problem when it's actually a visual problem. Walking into an Asian supermarket is exciting because of the shelves — rows of colourful packaging you want to pick up and turn over. No website captures that feeling. They give you text lists, Amazon grids, or subscription boxes where someone else picks for you.

Troy brings two things nobody else in this space has together: decades of genuine tasting experience and the design instinct to present snacks the way they deserve to be seen. He's not the face of the project — the shelf is. But the curation, the flavour knowledge, and the visual standard behind every shelf? That's 20 years of walking the aisles.

### Core Values

**The shelf is the product.** Everything in Konbini Scout exists to serve the shelf experience. If a feature doesn't make the shelf more beautiful, more useful, or more shareable, it doesn't ship. Every design decision, every data model choice, every interaction should be evaluated against: "does this make the shelf better?"

**Five is the magic number.** Constraint breeds clarity. Five snacks per shelf, five lucky cats per rating, savoury or sweet as a binary entry point. The entire product is built on the principle that fewer, better choices beat infinite scroll. This constraint is what makes participation accessible — anyone can pick 5.

**Appreciate, never appropriate.** Konbini Scout celebrates Asian snack culture with genuine respect. The tone is enthusiastic and knowledgeable, never performative or fetishising. If a description wouldn't feel right being read aloud in the store where that snack is sold, it doesn't belong on the site.

**Show up before you sign up.** The first shelf should appear within seconds of landing. No gates, no onboarding flows, no "sign up to see results." The magic happens when someone sees an illustrated shelf for the first time — don't put anything between them and that moment.

**Community over content.** The database seeds the experience, but user-generated shelves are what make it alive. Every feature should nudge users from browsing toward rating, curating, and sharing. The loop is: discover → buy → taste → rate → shelf → share.

### Strategic Pillars

**Visual-first, always.** When in doubt, choose the option that looks better on the shelf. Konbini Scout is a visual product competing in a text-heavy world. The illustrated shelf is the defensible differentiator — protect and invest in it.

**Low barrier, high reward.** Every interaction should be completable in under 10 seconds. Pick savoury or sweet. Choose a flavour. See 5 snacks. Tap one. Rate it. Build your shelf. Share it. No step should require explanation.

**Organic growth through sharing.** The product markets itself through shared shelves. Every feature should ask: "is this shareable?" If someone can't screenshot or link to it, it's not done.

**Content depth before audience breadth.** Don't chase users until the database feels rich. A small, well-stocked library of snacks with great illustrations and enthusiastic descriptions beats a massive but shallow catalogue every time.

### Success Looks Like

In 12 months, Konbini Scout has a rich database of 200+ Asian snacks across savoury and sweet, with hand-illustrated shelf art for every product. The site is live and growing organically — shared shelves circulate on Instagram and TikTok without any paid promotion. A few thousand users have signed up, built personal shelves, and are actively rating snacks. The community has generated enough data that the "community shelf" for popular categories genuinely reflects collective taste. Troy is adding new snacks weekly, the rating data is influencing which snacks get featured, and the first conversations about curated snack box mailouts are happening. The product feels alive — not like a static database, but like a shop that's always getting new stock on the shelf.

## 2. User Research

### Primary Persona

**Sam, 27, graphic designer in Melbourne.** Sam loves food content on TikTok — especially those "trying Japanese snacks for the first time" videos. She's been to a few Asian supermarkets with friends and bought random things off the shelf, but half the time she can't read the packaging and doesn't know what she's getting. She ended up with a bag of dried squid once when she thought it was chips. That was a learning experience.

Sam knows she likes matcha, anything spicy, and chewy/mochi textures. But she can't translate those preferences into specific products or brands. She browses r/JapaneseSnacks occasionally but the recommendations are scattered and she can never find them again when she's actually in the store. She's tried Bokksu once but cancelled after two months — she wanted to choose, not be chosen for.

Sam is digitally native, visually driven, and shares things that look good. If she found a site that showed her 5 matcha snacks displayed on a cute illustrated shelf, she'd screenshot it and send it to three friends immediately. She doesn't need to be convinced that Asian snacks are good — she needs help knowing which ones to grab.

### Secondary Personas

**Kai, 34, Korean-Australian software developer.** Grew up eating Asian snacks and knows the brands intimately. Kai's interest in Konbini Scout is the community and curation layer — he wants to build shelves of his favourites and see how his taste compares to the wider community. He's the experienced voice who rates confidently and whose shared shelves carry credibility.

**Priya, 22, university student and food content creator.** Priya makes TikTok videos about trying new foods. She's always looking for structured, visually appealing content hooks. A "My Top 5 Spicy Asian Snacks" shelf is exactly the kind of thing she'd turn into a video — and link back to in her bio. She's a potential bridge between Konbini Scout and a wider audience.

### Jobs To Be Done

**Functional Jobs:**
- When I'm in an Asian supermarket, I want to know which 5 things to grab so that I don't waste money on snacks I won't enjoy.
- When I discover a flavour I love, I want to find more snacks in that flavour family so that I can keep exploring in a direction I know I like.
- When I try a snack, I want to record my opinion so that I can remember what I've tried and build my personal collection.

**Emotional Jobs:**
- I want to feel like a confident explorer, not an ignorant outsider, when I walk into an Asian grocery store.
- I want the excitement of discovery — that feeling of finding something amazing that nobody in my friend group knows about yet.
- I want to feel part of a community of people who share my curiosity, not alone in my exploration.

**Social Jobs:**
- I want to be the friend who always has great snack recommendations so that I'm known as the person with interesting taste.
- I want to share my discoveries in a way that looks good and reflects my personality, not just a text message saying "try this."
- I want to see what other people rate highly so I can discover things I wouldn't have found on my own.

### Pain Points

**1. Overwhelming choice with no guidance (Severe, frequent).** Walking into an Asian supermarket means facing hundreds of unfamiliar products in languages you can't read. There's no equivalent of "staff picks" or "if you like X try Y." Users currently deal with this by buying randomly, asking friends, or leaving empty-handed. The consequence is missed discoveries and wasted money on products they don't enjoy.

**2. No visual, browseable reference (Moderate, frequent).** When users search "best Japanese snacks" online, they get text-heavy listicles with stock photos, Amazon grids, or YouTube videos they can't skim. There's nothing that replicates the feeling of scanning a shelf with your eyes. Users cope by bookmarking dozens of tabs they never revisit.

**3. Can't map flavour preferences to products (Moderate, frequent).** A user knows they love matcha-flavoured things but can't find a list of the best matcha snacks across all makers. Existing resources are organised by brand, country, or "top 10 overall" — not by flavour. Users deal with this by trial and error, which is slow and expensive.

**4. Recommendations are scattered and not persistent (Minor, frequent).** Someone recommends a snack on Reddit or TikTok. The user thinks "I should try that." Three days later, they can't find the post. There's no single place to save, collect, and act on snack recommendations.

### Current Alternatives & Competitive Landscape

**Snack subscription boxes (Bokksu, TokyoTreat):** These are the closest thing to curated Asian snack discovery. They do well at introducing variety — you get snacks you'd never find on your own. But you have zero control over what arrives, you can't browse by flavour or maker, and you're locked into a monthly spend whether you want it or not. Switching away means losing access to the curation entirely. They solve discovery through randomness, not guidance.

**Reddit and TikTok communities:** Great for authentic, passionate recommendations from real people. But the content is ephemeral and chaotic — a great recommendation from six months ago is effectively gone. You can't search by flavour, there's no visual browsing, and quality varies wildly. Discovery happens by accident, not design.

**Amazon and specialty retail sites:** They have the products but zero curation or personality. You get a product image, a description translated by someone who doesn't eat snacks, and 47 reviews that say "arrived on time." There's no way to browse by flavour, no shelf experience, no community. It's shopping, not discovery.

**Doing nothing:** The default. Users walk into stores and buy randomly, or they stick to the 2-3 snacks they already know. This is the real competitor — the inertia of "I don't know where to start, so I'll just get Pocky again."

### Key Assumptions to Validate

**1. "The illustrated shelf is more compelling than product photos."** We assume users will prefer a stylised, illustrated shelf view over a grid of real product images for browsing. To validate: A/B test shelf illustrations vs product photos with 50 users and measure engagement (time on page, taps into detail).

**2. "Flavour-first navigation maps to how people actually think about snacks."** We assume users think "I want something matcha" before they think "I want something by Calbee." To validate: Track which entry path (flavour vs maker) gets more traffic in the first 30 days.

**3. "5 items is the right constraint for participation."** We assume 5 is small enough to be accessible but large enough to feel meaningful. To validate: Monitor shelf completion rates. If users consistently build shelves of 3 or fewer, the constraint may be too high.

**4. "Users will rate snacks they've tried in real life, not just browse."** We assume the discover → buy → taste → rate loop will actually happen. To validate: Track the ratio of users who browse vs users who rate at least one snack within 30 days.

**5. "Shared shelves will drive organic growth."** We assume people will share their shelves on social media and those links will bring new users. To validate: Track referral traffic from shared shelf URLs in the first 60 days. If sharing is low, the shelf share UX may need to be more prominent.

**6. "Western snack explorers are a large enough audience."** We assume there are enough curious Western snackers to build a meaningful community. To validate: Monitor sign-up rate and geographic distribution in the first 90 days. If growth stalls below 500 users, the audience definition may need broadening.

### User Journey Map

**Awareness:** Sam sees a friend's Instagram story — a screenshot of a colourful illustrated shelf titled "My Top 5 Matcha Snacks" with 5 waving lucky cats. The visual is eye-catching enough to swipe up. She lands on the shared shelf page.

**Consideration:** The shelf is beautiful — illustrated snack packages arranged like a real store shelf, each with a lucky cat rating. Sam doesn't need to sign up to see it. She taps a snack and sees a real product photo, an enthusiastic description ("This one SLAPS"), flavour notes, and maker info. She's intrigued. She notices the "Browse by Flavour" and "Browse by Maker" options and starts exploring. Friction point: if the site feels thin (not enough snacks), she'll bounce here.

**First use:** Sam browses Sweet → Matcha and sees 5 illustrated snacks on the discovery shelf. She recognises one she's tried before. She taps it, reads the description, and thinks "yes, that's exactly right." She creates an account (quick social login) and rates it — 4 happy lucky cats. She now has one rating and the beginning of a personal shelf.

**Magic moment:** Sam opens a shared shelf link from someone she doesn't know — "Top 5 Spicy Rice Crackers." She sees 5 snacks she's never heard of, all with enthusiastic descriptions and high lucky cat ratings. She screenshots it and texts her friend: "we need to go to the Asian supermarket this weekend." The product just drove a real-world action.

**Habit formation:** After her supermarket trip, Sam comes back to rate the 3 snacks she bought. Two were great (5 cats), one was meh (2 grumpy cats). Her personal Matcha shelf now has 3 entries. She checks the community shelf for Matcha and sees her top pick is also the community's #2. She feels validated. She builds a "Sam's Sweet Picks" shared shelf and posts it to her Instagram story.

**Advocacy:** Sam's shared shelf link brings 4 friends to Konbini Scout. Two of them sign up and start rating. The loop continues. Sam is now checking in weekly to see what's new and how the community shelves are shifting.

## 3. Product Strategy

### Product Principles

**Shelf-first design.** Every screen should be one or two taps away from a shelf of 5. The shelf is the atomic unit of Konbini Scout — not the individual snack, not the search result, not the user profile. If you can't see a shelf, you're too deep.

**Instant visual gratification.** The first thing a user sees should be beautiful and complete — a populated shelf with illustrated snacks and lucky cat ratings. Never show an empty state as a first impression. The discovery shelf is always full.

**Constrained by design.** 5 snacks per shelf. Savoury or sweet. Flavour or maker. Lucky cats not stars. Every constraint is deliberate — it reduces cognitive load, increases shareability, and makes participation accessible. Resist the temptation to add "show more" or "see all results."

**The loop is the product.** Discover → buy → unbox → taste → rate → shelf → share. Every feature should serve this loop. If a feature doesn't move users along this path, question whether it belongs in the MVP.

**Personality over polish.** An enthusiastic, opinionated snack description matters more than pixel-perfect spacing. The product should feel like it was made by someone who genuinely loves snacks, not by a design committee. Ship personality first, polish second.

### Market Differentiation

Konbini Scout is the only product in the Asian snack space that combines three things nobody else has together: visual shelf-based browsing, flavour-first navigation, and constrained community curation.

Subscription boxes solve discovery through randomness — you get what they send. Social media solves it through chaos — you stumble on recommendations you can't find again. Retail sites don't solve it at all — they're shopping interfaces, not discovery tools.

Konbini Scout solves discovery through structure and beauty. The illustrated shelf makes browsing feel like being in a store. The savoury/sweet → flavour/maker navigation maps to how humans actually think about snacks. And the 5-item constraint turns curation from an expert activity into something anyone can do — you don't need to know 50 snacks, you just need to pick your top 5.

This is defensible because the combination of illustrated shelf art, curated content, and community data creates a flywheel: more users → more ratings → better community shelves → more shared shelves → more users. A competitor would need to replicate the content library, the illustration style, the community, and the data simultaneously.

### Magic Moment Design

The magic moment is: **someone opens a shared shelf link and instantly gets the concept, wants to try the snacks, and wants to build their own shelf.**

For this to happen reliably, these things must be true:

1. **The shared shelf page loads instantly** — no sign-up wall, no loading spinner, the illustrated shelf renders immediately.
2. **The shelf looks beautiful** — 5 illustrated snacks arranged on a shelf with lucky cat ratings. The visual impact is immediate and screenshot-worthy.
3. **Each snack is tappable** — one tap reveals the real photo, enthusiastic description, and flavour details. The depth is there if you want it.
4. **The "build your own" prompt is visible but not pushy** — a subtle nudge like "Rate snacks you've tried to build your own shelf" appears without blocking the experience.
5. **Social sharing metadata works perfectly** — when someone shares a shelf URL on any platform, the preview card shows the illustrated shelf with the shelf title. This is what drives the viral loop.

The shortest path from landing to magic moment is one click: open a shared shelf link. The shortest path from sign-up to creating value is: browse → find a snack you've tried → rate it → see it appear on your personal shelf. That should take under 60 seconds.

### MVP Definition

**Browse by Savoury/Sweet → Flavour or Maker → Shelf Display.** The core navigation funnel. User picks savoury or sweet, then browses by flavour category or maker. Top 5 results appear as illustrated products on a virtual shelf. This is the entry point for all discovery. Done: a user can go from the homepage to a shelf of 5 in 3 taps.

**Snack Detail Page.** Tap any snack on a shelf to see the full detail: real product photo, enthusiastic description, flavour notes, maker info, lucky cat community rating. Done: every snack in the database has a complete, engaging detail page.

**User Accounts & Rating.** Sign up via Clerk (Google/Apple social login). Rate any snack 1-5 lucky cats (grumpy cats for 1-2, neutral for 3, happy waving for 4-5). Done: a user can sign up in under 10 seconds and rate a snack in one tap.

**Personal Top 5 Shelf.** As users rate snacks, their top 5 per category auto-populates based on their highest ratings. Done: after rating 5+ snacks in a category, a user can see their personal shelf.

**Shared Shelves.** Any user can create a custom shelf of 5 recommended snacks, name it, and get a shareable link. Done: a user can build and share a shelf, and anyone with the link can view it without an account.

**Community Shelf.** Aggregate ratings from all users to show the community's top 5 per category. Done: when enough ratings exist for a category, the community shelf appears alongside the discovery shelf.

**Snack Database & Admin.** A way for Troy to add snacks to the database with: name, maker, flavour categories, savoury/sweet, description, product photo, and illustrated shelf image. Done: Troy can add a new snack in under 5 minutes.

### Explicitly Out of Scope

**Snack box e-commerce / payments.** Tempting because the snack box vision is part of the long-term plan, but payment infrastructure, shipping logistics, and inventory management are a completely separate product challenge. Deferred until the community is active and the demand is validated — reassess at 6 months.

**"Where to buy" links or affiliate integration.** Adding purchase links would be useful but introduces complexity (maintaining links, handling regional availability, affiliate program setup). The MVP should drive users to stores, not to checkout pages. Reconsider at 3 months.

**Mobile app.** The web-first decision is correct for sharing mechanics. A native app adds build complexity without solving the core problem. Reconsider when usage data shows significant mobile traffic patterns that the web app can't serve — likely 6-12 months.

**User-submitted snacks.** Letting users add snacks to the database opens up moderation, quality control, and duplicate management. Troy's curation is a feature, not a limitation. Reconsider when the content bottleneck becomes a growth blocker.

**Advanced social features.** Comments, following users, activity feeds, direct messaging. These are community features that matter when you have a community. The MVP needs to build the community first through the shelf sharing loop. Reconsider at 6 months.

**Search.** A text search bar seems obvious but is deferred intentionally. The product's differentiator is structured browsing (savoury/sweet → flavour/maker), not search. Adding search creates an alternative path that bypasses the shelf experience. Reconsider after launch if users request it.

### Feature Priority (MoSCoW)

**Must Have (P0):**
- Savoury/Sweet binary entry point
- Browse by Flavour navigation with shelf display
- Browse by Maker navigation with shelf display
- Illustrated shelf of 5 snacks as the core display unit
- Snack detail page with real photo and description
- Lucky cat rating system (1-5, grumpy/neutral/happy)
- User authentication (Clerk social login)
- Personal Top 5 shelf per category
- Shared Shelves (create, name, share link)
- Community shelf (aggregate top 5 per category)
- Admin capability to add/edit snacks
- Open Graph / social sharing metadata for shelf URLs

**Should Have (P1):**
- Shelf comparison view (my shelf vs community shelf side by side)
- Responsive design optimised for mobile browsers
- Landing/homepage with featured shelves
- Onboarding nudge for first-time visitors
- Basic analytics (page views, shelf shares, sign-ups)

**Could Have (P2):**
- Snack of the week/month featured promotion
- Category browsing for sub-flavours (e.g. Spicy → Wasabi, Chilli, Szechuan)
- User profile page showing all their shelves and ratings
- Notification when a snack you rated makes the community top 5

**Won't Have (this time):**
- E-commerce / snack box ordering
- Where to buy links / affiliate integration
- Mobile native app
- User-submitted snacks
- Text search
- Comments, following, messaging
- Multi-language support

### Core User Flows

**Flow 1: Discovery Browse**
Trigger: User lands on homepage or browses from any page.
1. User chooses Savoury or Sweet
2. User chooses Browse by Flavour or Browse by Maker
3. User selects a specific flavour category (e.g. Matcha) or maker (e.g. Calbee)
4. Illustrated shelf of top 5 snacks displays
5. User taps a snack to see the detail page
Success criteria: User reaches a shelf of 5 within 3 taps from homepage. Shelf renders in under 2 seconds.

**Flow 2: Rate and Build Personal Shelf**
Trigger: User has tried a snack and wants to record their opinion.
1. User navigates to a snack (via browse or from a shelf)
2. User taps the lucky cat rating (1-5)
3. If not signed in, quick Clerk auth prompt appears
4. Rating saves and the snack appears on their personal shelf for that category
5. Personal shelf updates to show top 5 based on highest ratings
Success criteria: Rating a snack takes one tap (if signed in). Personal shelf reflects the rating immediately.

**Flow 3: Create and Share a Shelf**
Trigger: User wants to recommend snacks to someone.
1. User taps "Create a Shelf"
2. User names the shelf (e.g. "Troy's Spicy Picks")
3. User selects 5 snacks from the database
4. Shelf preview displays as an illustrated shelf
5. User taps "Share" and gets a URL
6. Anyone with the URL can view the shelf without an account
Success criteria: Creating a shared shelf takes under 2 minutes. Shared URL generates a rich social preview card.

### Success Metrics

**Primary metric:** Shared shelf link clicks per week. This is the single number that matters most — it measures both content quality (are shelves worth sharing?) and growth potential (are shared shelves bringing new users?).
- Good: 100 link clicks/week by month 3
- Great: 500 link clicks/week by month 3

**Secondary metrics:**
- Snacks rated per active user per month. Good: 5. Great: 15.
- Personal shelves with 5+ ratings (shelf completion rate). Good: 30% of signed-up users. Great: 50%.
- Shared shelves created per week. Good: 20. Great: 50.

**Leading indicators:**
- Time to first shelf view (from landing). Target: under 10 seconds.
- Sign-up to first rating conversion. Target: 40% within first session.
- Return visits within 7 days. Good: 25%. Great: 40%.

### Risks

**1. Content bottleneck (High likelihood, High impact).** Troy is the sole content creator — every snack needs a description, photo, illustration, and categorisation. If adding snacks is slow, the database will feel thin and users will bounce. Mitigation: build a fast, frictionless admin flow. Prioritise breadth across categories over depth within categories. 10 flavour categories with 5 snacks each (50 total) is better than 2 categories with 25 each.

**2. Illustrated shelf art is expensive/slow to produce (Medium likelihood, High impact).** If each snack needs a custom illustration, scaling the database becomes an art production problem. Mitigation: develop a consistent illustration style that can be produced quickly — possibly a template-based approach or AI-assisted illustration with manual refinement. The style should be replicable.

**3. The rating loop doesn't close (Medium likelihood, High impact).** Users browse but don't buy snacks in real life, so they never come back to rate. The discover → buy → rate gap is the hardest part of the loop. Mitigation: make the unboxing and tasting experience a community hook. Consider a "trying this weekend" wishlist feature in a future iteration.

**4. Shared shelves don't generate enough social sharing (Medium likelihood, Medium impact).** The viral loop depends on people actually sharing shelf links. If they browse but don't share, organic growth stalls. Mitigation: make the shelf screenshot-worthy by default. Invest heavily in the visual quality of shared shelf preview cards (Open Graph images).

**5. Community shelves feel empty early on (High likelihood, Medium impact).** Until there are enough ratings, community shelves will be sparse or non-existent, which undermines the social comparison feature. Mitigation: seed with Troy's own ratings. Show discovery shelves (curated by the database) rather than community shelves until enough user data exists.

**6. Flavour categorisation is subjective (Low likelihood, Low impact).** Is "wasabi" a flavour or is "spicy" the category? Different users will categorise differently. Mitigation: Troy defines the canonical flavour taxonomy. Snacks can belong to multiple flavour categories. Keep the top-level categories broad (8-12 max) and add specificity later.

## 4. Brand Strategy

### Positioning Statement

For Western snack explorers who want to discover Asian snacks but feel overwhelmed by choice, Konbini Scout is the visual snack discovery platform that lets you browse, rate, and curate shelves of 5. Unlike subscription boxes that choose for you or social media where recommendations disappear, Konbini Scout puts you in control with an illustrated shelf experience that feels like walking through a beautifully designed convenience store.

### Brand Personality

Konbini Scout is your excitable friend who's been to every Asian supermarket in the city and can't wait to show you what they found. They're the person who grabs your arm in the snack aisle and says "oh my god, you HAVE to try this" — genuinely enthusiastic, never pretentious, a little bit chaotic in the best way.

They wear a vintage band tee with a konbini bag full of snacks. They'd never gatekeep — if you say "I've only had Pocky," they say "PERFECT, let me show you what's next." They speak in superlatives when they love something and are hilariously blunt when they don't. They take food seriously but never take themselves seriously.

They would never use corporate language, lecture someone about "authentic" snack culture, or make anyone feel dumb for not knowing a brand. They're the friend who makes discovering new things feel safe, exciting, and fun.

### Voice & Tone Guide

The voice is always **enthusiastic, opinionated, and casual** — like a passionate friend texting you about snacks. The tone shifts based on context but never loses its warmth and energy.

|---|---|---|
| **Onboarding** | "Let's find your new favourite snack. Sweet or savoury — what's calling you?" | "Welcome to Konbini Scout. Please select your preferred flavour category to begin browsing our database." |
| **Snack description** | "Imagine someone took the best cheese cracker you've ever had and gave it a Japanese engineering upgrade. Crispy, umami-loaded, dangerously addictive." | "A savoury cheese-flavoured cracker snack manufactured by Glico. Contains wheat flour and cheese powder." |
| **Error state** | "Hmm, that shelf fell over. Give it another try?" | "Error 500: Internal server error. Please try again later." |

### Competitive Differentiation Narrative

Every other product in the Asian snack space treats discovery as either a logistics problem or a content problem. Subscription boxes solve it with logistics — they ship you a box and hope you like what's inside. Social media solves it with content — someone films themselves eating snacks and you watch. Neither one puts the explorer in control.

Konbini Scout treats discovery as a design problem. The shelf — constrained to 5, illustrated, rated by lucky cats — is a design object that makes snack discovery feel like it does in real life: visual, tactile, and personal. You browse with your eyes. You pick what catches your attention. You build your own shelf the same way you'd arrange snacks on your desk after a shopping trip. Nobody else does this because nobody else has a creative director who's been eating Asian snacks for 20 years building the product.

### Brand Anti-Patterns

**Never sound corporate.** No words like "optimise," "leverage," "curate your experience," "our platform enables." If a sentence could appear in a SaaS landing page, rewrite it. Konbini Scout talks like a person, not a company.

**Never be weeby or performative.** Don't use gratuitous Japanese words for the sake of aesthetic. Don't use anime imagery. Don't describe snacks as "kawaii." The product name (Konbini) gets a pass because it directly references the store concept — beyond that, use Japanese terms only when they're the actual name of something (a snack, a maker, a flavour). Cultural appreciation means knowing the difference between celebration and costume.

**Never gatekeep or condescend.** Never imply a user should already know something. No "of course," no "obviously," no "any real snack fan knows." Every user is encountering this for the first time until proven otherwise. The tone is "let me show you something great," never "you haven't tried this?!"

**Never be visually generic.** No stock photos of smiling people eating snacks. No generic food photography with soft focus. No grid layouts that look like every other product database. The illustrated shelf is the signature — if a page could belong to any other food site, it's not done.

**Never prioritise completeness over personality.** A database with 50 snacks that each have an enthusiastic, opinionated description is better than a database with 500 snacks that have auto-generated copy. Every piece of text should sound like it was written by someone who ate the snack and has feelings about it.

## 5. Design Direction

### Design Philosophy

**The shelf is the canvas.** Every screen is designed around the shelf as the hero element. Backgrounds, typography, and spacing exist to make the shelf pop. The shelf should feel like a physical object — something you could reach into and grab a snack from.

**Illustrated meets real.** Two visual layers: illustrated, hand-drawn style for the shelf and browsing experience; real photography for the detail/depth layer. The transition from illustration to photo should feel like picking a product off the shelf and turning it over in your hands.

**Warm maximalism.** This is not a minimalist product. Asian snack packaging is colourful, bold, and busy — the design should honour that energy. But it's controlled maximalism: warm tones, retro textures, and plenty of visual detail contained within a clear layout structure.

**Personality in every pixel.** The lucky cats, the illustrated snacks, the enthusiastic copy — every element carries the brand voice. Even functional elements (loading states, empty states, error messages) should have personality.

### Visual Mood

The aesthetic is **retro Asian grocery** — think the warmth of a 1980s Japanese convenience store meets the visual language of vintage snack packaging. Hand-drawn illustrations with slightly imperfect lines, warm paper-like textures, and colours pulled from actual snack packaging: bold reds, warm yellows, matcha greens, soft creams.

Reference points: the illustration style of Japanese food packaging design, the warmth of Wes Anderson's colour palettes, the nostalgic feel of old-school konbini signage, and the playful energy of character mascots on Asian snack bags. It should feel like someone illustrated an entire convenience store and brought it to life on screen.

### Component Philosophy

**Border radius:** Generous and warm. Cards and containers: 12px (`--radius-lg`). Buttons and inputs: 8px (`--radius-md`). Small elements (badges, tags): 6px (`--radius-sm`). Pills (categories): 9999px (`--radius-full`).

**Shadows:** Soft, warm shadows that suggest depth without harshness. Cards: `0 2px 8px rgba(45, 42, 38, 0.08)`. Elevated (modals, dropdowns): `0 8px 24px rgba(45, 42, 38, 0.12)`. No sharp or coloured shadows.

**Borders:** 1px, using `--color-border`. Used sparingly — prefer shadow or background colour changes for visual separation. The shelf itself should feel borderless, like products sitting on a real surface.

**Buttons:** Rounded (8px radius), bold text. Primary: filled with `--color-primary`, white text. Secondary: outlined with `--color-border`, `--color-text`. Ghost: text only with `--color-primary`. All buttons have 12px vertical padding and 24px horizontal padding minimum.

**Inputs:** 8px radius, 12px padding, 1px border in `--color-border`. Focus state: 2px border in `--color-primary` with a soft glow `0 0 0 3px rgba(217, 79, 79, 0.15)`.

### Iconography & Imagery

**Icons:** Use Lucide icons (outline style). The clean, rounded line style complements the illustrated aesthetic without competing with it. Icons should be 20px default, 16px small, 24px large.

**Illustrations:** The signature element. Snack illustrations should be hand-drawn style with slightly imperfect lines, warm colours, and visible texture. They should look like they belong on vintage packaging — not flat vector art and not photorealistic. Think: the illustration on a jar of Japanese curry roux, or the character art on a bag of Umaibo.

**Photography:** Real product photos appear on the detail page only. Photos should be well-lit, on a neutral warm background (rice paper tone), showing the actual product packaging clearly. Troy's existing photo archive of finished packets can be used as-is for authenticity.

**Lucky cats:** The rating icons. Custom-illustrated maneki-neko in the same hand-drawn style as the snack illustrations. Three variants: happy waving (4-5 stars), neutral/calm (3 stars), grumpy with crossed arms (1-2 stars). These should be small enough to display in a row of 5 but detailed enough to be recognisable.

### Accessibility Commitments

**WCAG 2.1 AA compliance** as the baseline target.

**Colour contrast:** All text meets 4.5:1 contrast ratio against its background. Nori Dark (#2D2A26) on Rice Paper (#FAF6F0) = 12.5:1 (passes AAA). Interactive elements meet 3:1 against adjacent colours.

**Keyboard navigation:** All interactive elements are focusable and operable via keyboard. Shelf items can be navigated with arrow keys. Tab order follows visual order. Visible focus indicators on all interactive elements.

**Screen readers:** All images (including illustrated snacks) have descriptive alt text. Lucky cat ratings are announced as "rated 4 out of 5" not just the visual. Shelf titles and structure are semantically marked up with proper heading hierarchy.

**Touch targets:** Minimum 44x44px for all tappable elements. Lucky cat rating targets are large enough to tap individually on mobile.

**Reduced motion:** Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` media query. All animations should have a reduced or static alternative.

### Motion & Interaction

**Transition defaults:** Duration: 200ms for micro-interactions, 300ms for layout changes, 400ms for page transitions. Easing: `cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1)` for standard transitions. Spring-like easing for playful elements (lucky cats, shelf appearances).

**What animates:**
- Shelf items appearing (staggered fade-up, left to right, like products being placed on a shelf)
- Lucky cat rating interaction (selected cat does a subtle bounce/wave)
- Category pill selection (smooth background colour transition)
- Page transitions (subtle fade)
- Shared shelf loading (shelf slides into view)

**What doesn't animate:**
- Text content changes
- Navigation state changes
- Form submissions (use loading spinners instead)
- Error states (appear immediately for urgency)

**Hover states:** Shelf items scale up slightly (1.05) with a soft shadow increase on hover. Buttons darken slightly. Links underline.

**Loading approach:** Skeleton loaders that match the shelf layout. The shelf shape appears immediately (wooden shelf surface) with placeholder blocks where products will be. Products fade in as data loads. Never show a full-page spinner.
